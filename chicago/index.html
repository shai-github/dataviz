A Shrinking Middle Class in an Increasingly Polarized City

Since the Great Recession, the middle class has been losing ground across U.S. metropolitan areas. In Chicago, the erosion of the middle class started well before the economic downturn in 2008.
By Shai Slotky    March 12, 2022
Throughout the Midwest, the middle class as a share of the population has declined

Between 2000 and 2014, the median household income plummeted nationwide. As a result, the middle class decreased across metropolitan areas in the Rust Belt, where local economies have heavily relied on manufacturing. This shift represents an increase in income inequality, where - on average - the proportion of the lower and upper classes increased as the relative share of the middle class dropped.

Chicago's middle class has been waning over the past five decades

The decline of the middle class in Chicago - the third-most populous city in the United States - began well before the turn of the millennium. According to the Nathalie P. Voorhees Center for Neighborhood and Community Improvement at the University of Illinois at Chicago, the middle class comprised roughly half of the city's census tracts during the 1970s, while lower-income and higher-income communities constituted 42% and 8% of Chicago, respectively. By 2016, middle-income Chicago accounted for just 16% of the city's census tracts while the lower-income and higher-income shares increased to 62% and 22%, respectively.

Chicago's middle class has been pushed to the outskirts

While middle-income communities accounted for roughly half of Chicago in 1970, today - on average - most neighborhoods in the city tend to be either low-income or high-income. Using income categorization determined by the University of Illinois at Chicago, the following maps show this trend over time according to each tract's average per capita income. "Very High" income entails average per capita income 40 percent above the regional seven-county average while "High" income means 20 percent above the average. "Very Low" income tracts are those with average per capita income 40 percent below the average while "Low" income tracts are those with average per capita income 20 percent below the average. Lastly, "Middle" income tracts have average per capita income in a range 20 percent below and above the regional average.

Chicago, 1970

In 1970, middle-income areas made up roughly half of all neighborhoods in Chicago. Large groups of middle-income neighborhoods could be found on all sides of the city, though the northern Lake Michigan coast largely consisted of high-income neighborhoods. On the other hand, low-income neighborhoods were scattered across the south side of the city, with very low-income areas concentrated in the central-south and west.

Chicago, 1980

By 1980, there was an evident shift in the distribution of income in Chicago. Very low-income areas in the west and south sides of the city expanded further in each direction. Several middle-income tracts in the north became low-income areas while some low and middle-income neighborhoods on the northern coast became high-income neighborhoods.

Chicago, 1990

During the decade leading up to 1990, many census tracts moved from the low-income category to the very low-income category - particularly on the south and west sides of Chicago. Several middle and low-income neighborhoods adjacent to high and very high-income neighborhoods near the northern coast became high-income areas while a number of middle-income tracts in the northwest corner of the city became low-income. The southeastern edge of the city transitioned from majority middle-income to majority low-income.

Chicago, 2000

2000 saw the single largest leap toward income segregation in Chicago. Some low-income areas adjacent to high-income areas transitioned to middle-income neighborhoods while a collection of middle-income neighborhoods in the north became high-income. Nevertheless, large swathes of middle-income tracts in the northwest and southeast corners of the city became low-income areas during this decade. A number of low-income neighborhoods transitioned to very low-income areas.

Chicago, 2010

In 2010, Chicago experienced a striking gain in high-income tracts. Middle-income as well as low and very low-income neighborhoods adjacent to high and very-high income neighborhoods on the north side transitioned to high-income areas. On the other hand, the proportion of low-income neighborhoods marginally decreased, though not to the same level prior to the preceding decade. Notably, middle-income tracts as a share of the population remained stable, though numerous low-income neighborhoods transitioned to very low-income areas.

Chicago, 2016

The figures for 2016 are derived from five year estimates published in the 2012-2016 ACS by the U.S. Census Bureau rather than a decennial census. However, the figures show a more recent decline of the middle-class in Chicago with a transition to high-income neighborhoods. Much of this has been attributed to an influx of high-income individuals moving into already high-income and middle-income areas adjacent to high-income neighborhoods. Ultimately, the gradual decline of the middle-class in Chicago led to staunch economic polarization. As of 2017, middle-income areas in Chicago are restricted to the outskirts of the city in addition to pockets that straddle low-income and high-income neighborhoods.

The erosion of the middle class engenders economic polarization

Based on the University of Toronto's Three Cities methodology, researchers at UIC segmented Chicago into three distinct locales. These "three cities" vary in characteristics related to race, age, ability, home ownership, household composition, and income, among other indicators. The census tracts that compose each of the three cities largely align with the the low, middle, and high-income distribution of Chicago, highlighting the income segregation of the city along demographic and socioeconomic lines.

Chicago's Three Cities

The researchers at UIC found that Chicago's outlined three cities experienced significant changes in average per capita income over the five-decade period between 1970 and 2016. Tracts in City One experienced a proportional 20 percent increase in regional income whereas areas in City Three experienced a 20 percent decrease. Neighborhoods in City Two experienced a decrease in proportional income less than 20 percent on average.

City One

With only 622,099 residents as of 2017, City One represents the smallest population of Chicago's three cities. The demographic composition of City One is 64% White, 13% Black or African American, 8% Asian, 13% Latinx, and 3% Other races. 45% of City One's White population earned more than $100,000 annually. The city is comprised of the largest share of individuals between the ages of 20 and 34. Half of all households in the city are only comprised of two individuals. 43.1% of residents in City One own their homes while 56.9% reside in rented properties. The unemployment rate in City One decreased to 4.5% in 2017 compared to 6.3% in 2010.

City Two

As of 2017, City Two had 770,277 residents with a demographic composion of 42% White, 24% Black or African American, 7% Asian, 25% Latinx, and 2% Other races. Most individuals residing in City Two are between the ages 35 and 64 and more than a third of households in the city have four or more people. Compared to the other two cities, home ownership is relatively on par with renting, with 50.5% of individuals residing in rented properties and 49.5% in owned properties. With 8.7% unemployment, City Two had unemployment nearly double that of City One in 2017, though this figure decreased from 11.0% in 2010.

City Three

City Three represents the largest share of Chicago's overall population with 1,344,751 residents. The demographic composition of City Three is 14% White, 42% Black or African American, 4% Asian, 39% Latinx, and 1% Other races. As of 2017, more than a third of the city's Black population earned less than $20,000 while more than half earned less than $35,000. Approximately half of all households in the city have four or more people. Of the 673,000 individuals below the age of 19 citywide, roughly 60% reside in City Three. However, the city also represents almost half of all individuals over the age of 65, such that City Three is home to both a young and aging population. The composition of home ownership and renting in City Three is similar to that of City One. The unemployment rate of City Three - 14.3% - was the largest of the three cities in 2017; however, this rate only represented a marginal decrease from 14.4% in 2010.
