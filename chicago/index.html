A Shrinking Middle Class in an Increasingly Polarized City

Since the Great Recession, the middle class has been losing ground across U.S. metropolitan areas. In Chicago, the erosion of the middle class started well before the economic downturn in 2008.
By Shai Slotky    March 12, 2022
Throughout the Midwest, the middle class as a share of the population has declined

Between 2000 and 2014, the median household income plummeted nationwide. As a result, the middle class decreased across metropolitan areas in the Rust Belt, where local economies have heavily relied on manufacturing. This shift represents an increase in income inequality, where - on average - the proportion of the lower and upper classes increased as the relative share of the middle class dropped.

The Pew Research Center defines "middle-income" as as adults whose annual household income is two-thirds to double the national median, after incomes have been adjusted for household size. The Pew Research Center defines "low-income" as households with incomes less than 67% of the median and upper-income households as having incomes that are more than double the median.

Chicago's middle class has been waning over the past five decades

The decline of the middle class in Chicago - the third-most populous city in the United States - began well before the turn of the millennium. According to the Nathalie P. Voorhees Center for Neighborhood and Community Improvement at the University of Illinois at Chicago, the middle class comprised roughly half of the city's census tracts during the 1970s, while lower-income and higher-income communities constituted 42% and 8% of Chicago, respectively. By 2016, middle-income Chicago accounted for just 16% of the city's census tracts while the lower-income and higher-income shares increased to 62% and 22%, respectively.

Researchers at the Voorhees Center defined "middle class" tracts as having an average per capita income between 80 and 120 percent of regional average for a given year. Tracts were only assessed if average per capita income was between $28,712 and $43,068. At the time of publishing, decennial U.S. Census data was not available and, as such, five year estimates from the 2012-2016 American Community Survey were used. Census tracts were analyzed according to 2010 U.S. Census boundaries.

Chicago's middle class has been pushed to the outskirts

While middle-income communities accounted for roughly half of Chicago in 1970, today - on average - most neighborhoods in the city tend to be either low-income or high-income. Using income categorization determined by the University of Illinois at Chicago, the following maps show this trend over time according to each tract's average per capita income. "Very High" income entails average per capita income 40 percent above the regional seven-county average while "High" income means 20 percent above the average. "Very Low" income tracts are those with average per capita income 40 percent below the average while "Low" income tracts are those with average per capita income 20 percent below the average. Lastly, "Middle" income tracts have average per capita income in a range 20 percent below and above the average.
